What is Radiography?

Radiography, commonly known as X-rays, is a medical imaging technique in which a very small dose of ionizing radiation (X-rays) is applied to the human body to produce images, called radiographs (x-ray images). The healthcare professional who practices radiography is called a Radiographer, or commonly referred to as X-ray technologist. Our job is to produce the best possible x-ray images for doctors to diagnose.

What are the differences between a Radiographer and a Radiologist?

Radiographer and Radiologist are different jobs.

A Radiographer is a health care professional trained in performing an X-ray procedure; a Radiologist is a medical doctor specializing in reading x-rays, ultrasounds, MRI, CT images to diagnose a patient.

Since Radiographers are NOT doctors, we cannot discuss findings we see on X-rays and we cannot diagnose the patient. This is because diagnosing the patient equates to practicing medicine, something only medical doctors can do. Our job as Radiographers are to produce the best X-rays images for doctors to diagnose.

What are the differences between a Radiographer and a Nurse?

Similar to the previous question, Nurses and Radiographers are different jobs with different responsibilities. Radiographers have the title MRT(R) and Nurses have the title RN or RPN.

For example:

  • Nurses are allowed to administer pain-killers to a patient, Radiographers may assist the Nurse by sitting the patient up but CANNOT administer the pain medication.
  • Radiographers are allowed to take X-rays, Nurses may assist the Radiographer by positioning the patient for an X-ray but CANNOT press the X-ray exposure button.
