Abdominal Abscess

Introduction

Abscess is a localized pocket of purulent exudate or pus in a solid tissue (Gould, 2011). Purulent exudate refers to the thick and yellow-green fluid that contains leukocytes and cell debris. When the immune system is triggered at the site of injury, immune cells leaks out of blood vessels via vasodilation to secret enzymes to kill invading bacteria. This creates a dead pool of cell debris and the process is called suppurative inflammation.

When an abscess develops in the abdominal cavity, it is called an abdominal abscess. Usually, abdominal abscess is a secondary illness that develops anatomically close to its primary illness. For example:

  • Perforated appendix → periappendiceal abscess
  • Severe pancreatitis → lesser sac abscess
  • Small bowel perforation → abscess develops anywhere from ligament of Treitz to ileum

If an abdominal abscess is left untreated, it is going to stimulate an influx of acute inflammatory response cells. When this happens, bacteria enters the bloodstream to cause bacteremia. Bacteremia can cause generalized sepsis shock.

Risks and Symptoms

As mentioned before, abdominal abscess is usually a secondary illness to a primary cause. These primary illnesses can be: weakened immune system, inflammatory bowel disease, bowel perforation, multiple organ failure, and recurrent abscesses.

Patients experiences abdominal discomfort and fever. However, because many pathologies can have abdominal discomfort as a symptom, medical imaging modalities such as CT and ultrasound are often used to differentiate abscess from other possible pathologies.

Patient presentation may vary based on primary illness. If the primary cause is abdominal distension, patient may present with a hugely dilated bowel.

Diagnostic Imaging

While plain AP Abdomen radiographs can show fluid levels and air in bowel, it does not show abscess. Abdominal abscess are usually shown on CT images. There are several CT patterns for abscess:

  • Enhanced ring: Vasculature around the abscess sac taking up CT contrast.
  • Locualted gass patterns within abscess sac: Bacteria inside the abscess metabolize and release gas.
  • Abnormal hyperdense region: Region around the bowel should appear hypodense because of mesentary is fatty. Any hyperdense region near the bowel region indicates abdnormalities.
  • Absence bowel wall pattern: Bowel wall pattern are visualized on normal bowel. Since Abscesses are not bowel, they do not have the bowel wall pattern.
