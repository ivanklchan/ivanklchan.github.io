Infective Endocarditis

Introduction

Infective endocarditis refers to the formation of nodules or vegetations on heart valves by deposits of bacteria or fungi (Eisenberg, 2015). Vegetation means any abnormal growth of a body part. While infective endocarditis affects the endocardium, it typically targets the endocardium of heart vavles. Infection is usually caused by the streptococci and staphylococci bacteria group.

Infective endocarditis is universally fatal is left untreated, this is because endocarditis leads to impaired cardiac function.

There are three types of infective endocarditis

  1. Subacute bacterial endocarditis

    2. Subacute bacterial endocarditis progresses slowly but it is aggressive. It is a secondary infection to a primary infection. Usually, the source of entry is not evident, but a primary infection that may cause subacute bacterial endocarditis sis oral infection.

  2. Acute bacterial endocarditis

    3. Acute bacterial endocarditis occurs when the body is exposed to a massive amount of virulent bacteria. In contrast to subacute bacterial endocarditis, acute bacterial endocarditis has a evident source of entry.

  3. Valvular endocarditis

    4. Valvular endocarditis happens when the heart valve is infected becaues of sterile equipment contamination during surgery. It affects the aortic valve more than the bicuspid valve. A prosthetic valve can also be infected as well.

Pathogenesis & Pathophysiology

Bacteria from the source of infection such as from GI tract, oral cavity, or contamination during sterile procedure adheres to the heart valves by circulation. After the heart valves are exposed to bacteria, it begins to inflames and grow larger in size. As a result of heart valve vegetation, the valve malfunctions, which leads to disrupted blood flow and low cardiac output. Low cardiac output eventually cause hypoxia. The vegetation from the heart valve can also break off and cause a stroke as it get sloughed in the cranial vessels.

Risks & Diagnosis

Bacteremia, prosthetic valve replacement, and a weak immune system can increase the risks of developping infective endocarditis.

Medical imaging diagnosis is usually made by transesophageal, transthoracic echocardiography, or heart CT. Blood culture can also make a definite diagnosis as well. It is almost impossible to diagnose infective endocarditis from plan chest radiographs.

Patient Presentation

Patients presenting with infective endocarditis can have high fever and showing signs of hypoxia such as chest pain and clubbing of fingers. They could also have nail bed haemorrhages and lesions in palms and soles of feet. There are two types of lesions:

  1. Osler's nodes: painful lesions.
  2. Janeway lesions: not painful lesions.
